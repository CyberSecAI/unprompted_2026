# Overview

## Summary
Lieutenant Colonel Chase Hasbrouck from US Army Cyber Command discusses the friction of adopting AI in massive, highly regulated government enterprises compared to Silicon Valley.

## Key Takeaway
Scaling AI in the military requires overcoming multi-year procurement and accreditation cycles while managing a million-plus specialized, often part-time, workforce.

# Insights

## The Government vs. Silicon Valley Gap
- The Silicon Valley "API key and go" model is fundamentally incompatible with the military's rigorous "Authority to Operate" (ATO) framework.
- Government AI adoption is delayed by multi-year procurement cycles followed by equally lengthy security and privacy accreditation processes.
- A tool can easily take four years from selection to production approval, by which time the underlying technology and mission needs have changed.

## Managing a Million-Plus Workforce
- US Army Cyber Command oversees a diverse workforce of over one million people, including active duty, National Guard, Reserve, and civilians.
- Managing security controls for a workforce that is one-third part-time (Guard/Reserve) creates unique challenges for consistent AI policy enforcement.
- Personnel are drafted into extremely specialized, discrete roles where they are expected to follow strict, pre-defined career plans and timelines.

## Challenges of Enterprise AI Onboarding
- Initial efforts to integrate AI chatbots into government cybersecurity workflows have faced significant resistance and broad adoption failure.
- Technical "power users" are often pulled away from their primary missions to act as facilitators for struggling enterprise-wide AI rollouts.
- The primary barrier to AI success in large government orgs is the organizational inertia of massive, specialized teams rather than the technology itself.

## LLMs and Specialized Roles
- LLMs offer a major opportunity for a workforce that depends on natural language processing rather than manual coding or querying.
- Fitting general-purpose LLMs into highly specific military cyber roles requires a significant rethink of traditional training and operational workflows.
- The long-term goal is to shift the workforce from being "manual laborers" of data to managers of sophisticated, AI-driven offensive and defensive agents.

## The Fractal Nature of Classification
- AI security requirements are "fractal," multiplying in complexity as data moves from unclassified to Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI).
- Transitioning AI tools to "Secret" and above classified networks increases the technical and security overhead by an order of magnitude.
- Operational insights from classified environments are difficult to share, creating a knowledge silo that slows broader organizational AI maturity.

## AI-Driven Reverse Engineering
- Unlimited compute and tokens enable powerful new automated workflows for reverse engineering complex, proprietary software and systems.
- Integrating tools like Ghidra with AI (via MCP) transforms malware analysis from a slow, manual craft into a scalable, automated process.
- These advanced RE workflows represent a significant capability leap that is often restricted by organizational and classification boundaries.

## Scaling Incident Response
- AI is essential for bridging the gap between small, elite IR teams and the massive scale of the Army's global digital footprint.
- Lessons from major events like SolarWinds prove that manual, hands-on forensics cannot keep pace with modern, automated enterprise-wide threats.
- Automating the forensic and malware analysis pipelines is a critical strategic priority for protecting the Army's diverse digital infrastructure.

## Failure of Static Governance
- The traditional 4-year cycle for procurement and accreditation is fundamentally broken in the era of weekly AI feature releases.
- Rapidly evolving AI requires a dynamic governance model that can provide risk-informed ownership at the speed of the technology.
- Static, multi-year plans are being rendered obsolete, requiring a cultural shift toward proactive, continuous risk management and adoption.
